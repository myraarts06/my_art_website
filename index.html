ABOUT ME

My name is Pradeep Krishna. I am a Frankfurt-based, research-driven contemporary conceptual artist. I work across painting, photography, and installation. My path into art comes from a prior academic and professional background in Pharmacy and Ecotoxicology, followed by more than a decade of research and pharmacovigilance within the corporate sector. This experience shaped a way of thinking rooted in observation, systems, and the analysis of complex interdependent structures. This is the approach that continues to inform my artistic practice today. My transition into art reflects a shift in method rather than a departure from inquiry. At the center of my practice is a sustained investigation into the nature of self and perception, articulated through the question: Who am I? Working in Indian philosophical traditions and contemporary contexts, my practice explores questions that transcend cultural boundaries while remaining rooted in lived experience. Indian Knowledge Systems function as a lived intellectual and contemplative framework within my practice, informing how I approach consciousness, perception, and experience as embodied and observable phenomena rather than abstract concepts. These systems function not only as subjects of study but as practical frameworks that shape how questions are developed, researched, and translated into visual form. My practice unfolds in distinct series-based bodies of work. Each series emerges from a specific line of inquiry, currently including the concept of the Three Guṇas — Sattva, Rajas, and Tamas — as a framework for exploring shifting states of human experience. Through exhibitions, installations, publications, and interdisciplinary collaborations, I seek to develop projects that connect artistic practice with broader cultural, philosophical, and institutional conversations. And engage with an international audience. Across mediums, I am interested in producing my work that seeks to create visual spaces that operate between image and encounter — where perception is not only represented but activated. While painting currently forms the core of my practice, I am increasingly working toward installation-based formats that expand the experiential and spatial dimensions of this inquiry. Ultimately, my practice seeks to create spaces where the viewer is invited into reflection, not conclusion.

Artist statement

My practice investigates the nature of self and human perception through visual form. In an age increasingly defined by distraction, acceleration, and externalized forms of attention, my work investigates the conditions through which experience, perception, and identity are shaped. Drawing from Indian Knowledge Systems, I use visual art to explore questions of selfhood and consciousness that remain fundamental to human experience across cultures and historical periods. Rather than using art to just illustrate pre-existing ideas, I use it as a mode of inquiry in which understanding emerges through the process of making. Sometimes meaning is not fully formed before the work begins; it develops through decisions of composition, material interaction, and perceptual testing during the act of creation itself. In this sense, the studio becomes a space where thinking happens through seeing and making, rather than preceding it. My background in Pharmacy and Ecotoxicology, along with long-term work in pharmacovigilance, has shaped a way of engaging with systems, observation, and gradual transformation. This influences how I construct visual works — as layered structures where relationships between elements shift under attention rather than remain fixed. Indian Knowledge Systems provide a lived contemplative framework through which I approach perception and consciousness as experiential phenomena. Within this context, concepts such as the Three Guṇas — Sattva, Rajas, and Tamas — operate not as symbolic references, but as functional lenses for examining states of human experience. My work moves between painting, photography, and installation. Painting allows me to construct controlled visual fields through layering, contrast, and compositional tension. Photography extends this inquiry into lived environments and temporal conditions, while installation opens the possibility of constructing spatial situations where perception is shaped by movement and presence. Across all mediums, I work with formal elements such as rhythm, density, fragmentation, light, and spatial ambiguity to create shifts in perceptual attention. These are not aesthetic choices alone, but investigative tools through which perception is tested and reorganized. The viewer is not positioned as a passive observer, but as part of the perceptual system that the work activates. Meaning emerges through the act of encountering the work rather than being contained within it. The aim of my practice is not to resolve inquiry, but to sustain it — allowing perception itself to remain active, unstable, and open within the experience of the work.

PROJECT OVERVIEW

3 Guṇas is an ongoing body of work that explores the fundamental qualities of nature according to the Indian philosophical traditions (Sankhya and Yoga). These are the same forces that shape human experience: Sattva (clarity), Rajas (activity), and Tamas (inertia).

Rather than approaching these concepts as philosophical abstractions, the project investigates them as lived conditions that influence perception, emotion, attention, and identity. Through painting, the series examines how these forces manifest in contemporary life and how they shape our experience of ourselves and the world around us.

CENTRAL QUESTION

While the individual works focus on different aspects of human experience, the project as a whole asks a broader question: How do the forces that shape experience obscure or reveal the nature of the Self?

3 Guṇas

Ahambrahmasmi

Aham Brahmasmi serves as the central work within the 3 Guṇas series.

The title, drawn from the Upanishadic Mahāvākya meaning “I am Brahman,” refers to a state beyond identification with the shifting conditions of experience. Within the framework of the series, the work points toward a dimension of selfhood that exists beyond Sattva, Rajas, and Tamas.

Rather than depicting a philosophical conclusion, the painting functions as a visual meditation on transcendence, inviting reflection on the relationship between human experience and the nature of the Self.
